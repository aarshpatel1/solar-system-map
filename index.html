The Sun

Basic Facts

  • Type: G-type main-sequence star (G2V), commonly called a yellow dwarf.
  • Age: Approximately 4.6 billion years old.
  • Diameter: About 1.39 million kilometers (864,000 miles).
  • Mass: Roughly 1.989 x 1030 kilograms, accounting for about 99.86% of the total mass of the Solar System.

Structure

  • Core: The central region where nuclear fusion occurs, converting hydrogen into helium and releasing vast amounts of energy.
  • Radiative Zone: The layer where energy produced in the core is radiated outward.
  • Convective Zone: The outer layer of the Sun's interior where energy is transferred by convection.
  • Photosphere: The visible surface of the Sun, emitting the light we see.
  • Chromosphere: A layer above the photosphere, visible during solar eclipses.
  • Corona: The outermost part of the Sun's atmosphere, extending millions of kilometers into space and visible during a total solar eclipse.

Solar Activity

  • Sunspots: Dark, cooler areas on the Sun's surface caused by magnetic activity.
  • Solar Flares: Sudden eruptions of energy and particles from the Sun's surface.
  • Coronal Mass Ejections (CMEs): Large expulsions of plasma and magnetic field from the corona into space.

Influence on the Solar System

  • Solar Wind: A continuous flow of charged particles from the Sun that affects the entire Solar System and shapes planetary magnetospheres.
  • Heliosphere: The bubble-like region of space dominated by the solar wind and magnetic field, extending far beyond Pluto's orbit.

Life Cycle

Currently in its stable phase, the Sun will eventually evolve into a red giant, expanding significantly before shedding its outer layers and leaving behind a white dwarf.

The Sun is essential not only for providing the energy necessary for life on Earth but also for driving the dynamics of the entire Solar System.
